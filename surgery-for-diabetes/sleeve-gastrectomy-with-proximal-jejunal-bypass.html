Understanding The Procedure

How Sleeve with Proximal Jejunal Bypass Works

Sleeve Gastrectomy with Proximal Jejunal Bypass (SG-PJB) is a state-of-the-art hybrid operation designed to maximize metabolic disease reversal while maintaining anatomical simplicity. It synergizes two distinct surgical mechanisms into one powerful procedure.

Step 1: The Gastric Sleeve (Ghrelin Reset)

The surgeon removes approximately 75% of the stomach along its greater curvature, creating a narrow sleeve. Crucially, this removes the gastric fundus—the primary site where ghrelin (the hunger hormone) is produced. Appetite drops immediately, and portion control becomes effortless.

Step 2: Proximal Jejunal Bypass (Incretin Surge)

A measured segment of the upper small intestine (proximal jejunum) is bypassed by connecting a loop of lower bowel to the duodenum or upper jejunum. By rerouting food past the proximal bowel directly to the distal small intestine (hindgut stimulation), specialized L-cells release a massive surge of natural GLP-1 and PYY hormones.

Breaking Cellular Insulin Resistance

The combination of ghrelin reduction and GLP-1 surge breaks cellular insulin resistance within hours. Fasting blood sugars stabilize rapidly in the hospital, allowing most patients to cut or discontinue insulin injections before discharge.

Candidate Screening

Are You an Ideal Candidate for SG-PJB?

According to international metabolic surgery guidelines (DSS-II and IFSO/ASMBS), SG-PJB is highly recommended for individuals with Type 2 Diabetes and a BMI ≥ 30 to 40+ kg/m² who need both substantial weight reduction and powerful hormonal glycemic control, especially when standard medications or lifestyle changes have fallen short.

Ask Dr. Peters
Why add a Proximal Jejunal Bypass to a Sleeve Gastrectomy?

A standalone Sleeve Gastrectomy is excellent for weight loss, but its diabetes remission rate (~60–65%) relies mostly on caloric restriction and weight reduction over time. By adding a **Proximal Jejunal Bypass**, food is rerouted to the lower small intestine immediately. This triggers an explosive natural release of GLP-1 and PYY incretin hormones, boosting diabetes remission rates to **80–85%** and stabilizing glucose within 48 hours.

Is SG-PJB safer than Roux-en-Y Gastric Bypass?

For many patients, SG-PJB offers a simplified anatomical profile with fewer long-term risks. Because the stomach is shaped into a sleeve without creating a blind remnant pouch or multiple complex anastomoses, diagnostic endoscopy of the stomach remains easily accessible in the future. Additionally, the risk of internal hernias and marginal ulcers is significantly lower compared to traditional Roux-en-Y bypass.

Will I experience severe diarrhea or nutritional deficiencies?

No. SG-PJB is carefully calibrated to bypass only the proximal jejunum while keeping the majority of the small intestine active for nutrient absorption. Patients maintain healthy protein, calcium, and iron levels with standard daily bariatric multivitamins, and bowel movements typically remain smooth and regular without severe diarrhea.

How soon can I stop taking insulin after SG-PJB?

Due to the immediate incretin hormone surge occurring when food hits the lower intestine, blood sugar levels begin dropping within **24 to 48 hours after surgery**. Most patients dependent on insulin have their doses reduced by 50% to 100% before leaving the hospital, achieving complete independence from injections within weeks.

Is SG-PJB covered by health insurance in India?

Yes. Under **IRDAI guidelines**, metabolic and bariatric surgical procedures are covered by health insurance policies in India for individuals meeting BMI and comorbidity criteria (such as BMI ≥ 32.5 kg/m² with Type 2 Diabetes). Max Hospital offers seamless cashless processing with all major insurance networks and TPAs.

What is the recovery timeline after laparoscopic SG-PJB?

Performed laparoscopically or robotically through 3 to 5 tiny keyhole incisions, recovery is rapid. Patients walk within 4 hours of surgery, stay in the hospital for **1 to 2 nights**, and can return to routine non-strenuous desk work and daily activities within 5 to 7 days.
