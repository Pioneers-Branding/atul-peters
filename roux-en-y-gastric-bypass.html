Procedure Overview

What is Roux-en-Y Gastric Bypass?

Roux-en-Y Gastric Bypass (RYGB) is a combined restrictive and malabsorptive procedure that has been performed for over 60 years. It is widely considered the "Gold Standard" against which all other bariatric operations are measured.

The surgery involves creating a small stomach pouch (about the size of an egg) and connecting it directly to the small intestine, bypassing a significant portion (100-170 cm) of the stomach and upper intestine.

This not only restricts food intake but also reduces calorie absorption and triggers powerful hormonal changes that improve metabolism, reduce hunger, and reset the body's weight set-point.

The Procedure

How Gastric Bypass Works

A precise, minimally invasive surgery that creates a new pathway for digestion

The New Anatomy

  • New Pouch (30ml) — Egg-sized stomach that limits food intake
  • Y-Junction — Food joins intestinal bypass here
  • Bypassed Stomach — Still produces enzymes, not removed
01

Pre-Surgery Preparation

A comprehensive team evaluation including body composition analysis, dietary counseling, physician review, pulmonologist and cardiologist clearance, and psychiatric assessment. Thromboembolism prophylaxis is administered 6-12 hours before surgery.

02

Positioning & Anesthesia

You are placed in the reverse Trendelenberg position under general anesthesia. This specialized positioning allows optimal access to the upper abdomen for the surgical team.

03

Small Incisions

The surgeon makes 3-4 small incisions (5-12mm) in the abdomen. These tiny ports accommodate the laparoscopic camera and specialized instruments. In 99% of cases, incisions are closed with medical glue — no stitches or bandages required.

04

Creating the Pouch

A small gastric pouch (about 30-50ml — the size of an egg) is created at the top of the stomach using surgical staplers. A calibration tube ensures precise sizing. The remainder of the stomach is separated but left in place.

05

Bypass the Intestine

The small intestine is divided, and 100-170 cm is bypassed. The lower portion of the intestine is then connected directly to the new stomach pouch, allowing food to skip the bypassed segment.

06

Reconnection & Closure

The Y-shaped reconnection allows bile and pancreatic juices to mix with food further down the intestine. The new anatomy is secured with staples or sutures, and the small incisions are sealed with glue.

01

Restriction

The small stomach pouch dramatically reduces the amount of food you can eat at one time. You feel full after just a few bites, naturally limiting caloric intake.

02 · Key

Malabsorption

By bypassing 100-170 cm of small intestine, the body absorbs fewer calories and nutrients from food — particularly fats and sugars — supporting significant weight loss.

03

Hormonal Reset

Powerful changes in gut hormones including ghrelin (hunger), GLP-1 and PYY (satiety), and GIP (insulin response) — improving metabolism, reducing hunger, and resetting your body's weight set-point.

Post-Surgery Diet

Phase I: Clear Liquid Diet

The first step in your recovery — a structured clear liquid phase

✓ Do's

  • Drink clear liquids only — water, broth, sugar-free gelatin
  • Sip slowly throughout the day (60-90 ml at a time)
  • Aim for 64+ oz of fluid daily
  • Stop immediately if you feel full, pain or discomfort
  • Take prescribed vitamin and mineral supplements

✗ Don'ts

  • Avoid sweetened beverages (calories + dumping syndrome risk)
  • No caffeinated drinks (can cause dehydration)
  • No carbonated beverages (can stretch the pouch)
  • No alcoholic drinks (absorbed faster, higher intoxication)
  • Never use straws (introduces air, causes discomfort)
