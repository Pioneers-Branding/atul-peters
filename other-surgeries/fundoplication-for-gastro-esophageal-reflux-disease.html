Understanding The Procedure

How Fundoplication Stops Acid Reflux

Gastroesophageal Reflux Disease (GERD) occurs when the Lower Esophageal Sphincter (LES) becomes weak or when a hiatal hernia pushes the stomach upward through the diaphragm. Medications like PPIs only neutralize acid—they do not repair the broken mechanical valve. Fundoplication restores normal anatomy permanently.

Step 1: Hiatal Hernia Repair (Crural Closure)

If part of the stomach has herniated into the chest cavity through the diaphragm, Dr. Atul Peters gently pulls it back down into its natural abdominal position. The enlarged opening in the diaphragm is then carefully sutured and reinforced to prevent re-herniation.

Step 2: The Fundoplication Wrap (360° or 270°)

The upper dome of the stomach (the gastric fundus) is mobilized and wrapped around the lower 2 to 3 cm of the esophagus. In a **Nissen Fundoplication**, a complete 360-degree wrap is performed; in a **Toupet Fundoplication**, a 270-degree partial wrap is created based on your esophageal motility.

Step 3: Creating a One-Way Valve

The wrap is securely sutured to the esophagus. Whenever the stomach contracts during digestion, the wrap contracts around the esophagus, reinforcing the valve. This allows food and liquid to pass down smoothly while creating a one-way physical barrier against upward reflux.

Clinical Indications

When Should You Consider Anti-Reflux Surgery?

Surgery is strongly recommended if you experience: 1) Persistent heartburn, regurgitation, or nocturnal choking despite taking PPI medications; 2) A confirmed **medium-to-large Hiatal Hernia** (> 3-5 cm); 3) Intolerance or unwillingness to take lifelong antacid drugs; or 4) Complications such as erosive esophagitis, esophageal strictures, or **Barrett's Esophagus**.

Common Questions

Get clear, evidence-based answers to the most frequently asked questions about Nissen and Toupet Fundoplication surgery.

Ask Dr. Peters
Why choose surgery over taking daily PPI pills like Pantoprazole?

Proton Pump Inhibitors (PPIs) only suppress stomach acid production; they do not fix the weak valve or repair a hiatal hernia. Therefore, bile and stomach fluids still reflux upward into your throat and lungs. Furthermore, lifelong PPI use is associated with serious side effects including bone fractures, kidney damage, and vitamin B12/magnesium deficiency. **Fundoplication physically repairs the valve**, providing a permanent cure without daily pills.

Will I have difficulty swallowing or belching after surgery?

During the first 2 to 4 weeks, mild temporary swallowing tightness (dysphagia) is common due to normal postoperative tissue swelling around the newly constructed valve. This resolves completely as swelling subsides. Some patients experience temporary "gas bloat" or difficulty belching, which is why following our structured liquid-to-soft diet progression and eating slowly is essential during early healing.

What tests are required before undergoing fundoplication?

A comprehensive diagnostic evaluation is mandatory to ensure excellent surgical outcomes: 1) **Upper GI Endoscopy** to assess esophagitis, hiatal hernia size, and rule out strictures; 2) **24-Hour Esophageal pH / Impedance Monitoring** to objectively measure acid and non-acid reflux episodes; and 3) **High-Resolution Esophageal Manometry** to test esophageal swallowing strength and rule out motility disorders like achalasia.

Can fundoplication cure or reverse Barrett's Esophagus?

Barrett's Esophagus is a precancerous change in the esophageal lining caused by chronic acid and bile burn. While surgery does not instantly erase existing Barrett's cells, **fundoplication stops 100% of acid and bile reflux**, halting further cellular damage and preventing progression to esophageal adenocarcinoma. In many cases, long-term regression of low-grade Barrett's is observed after successful surgery.

How long will the surgical wrap last? Can GERD return?

When performed by an expert minimal access surgeon like Dr. Atul Peters using precise suturing techniques, fundoplication is designed to last a lifetime. Over **90% to 95% of patients remain completely symptom-free at 10 to 15 years follow-up**. To protect your wrap long-term, we advise avoiding heavy weightlifting or severe straining during the first 3 months of recovery.
