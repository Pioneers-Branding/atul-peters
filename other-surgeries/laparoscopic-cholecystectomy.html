Understanding Gallstones

Why Symptomatic Gallstones Require Surgical Removal

The gallbladder is a small digestive pouch that stores bile produced by your liver. When cholesterol or bilirubin crystallizes inside this pouch, it forms hard deposits known as **gallstones (cholelithiasis)**.

The Danger of Duct Blockage & Pancreatitis

When a gallstone blocks the cystic duct after a fatty meal, it causes intense, cramping pain (**Biliary Colic**). If a stone escapes into the main bile duct, it can block bile flow (causing **Jaundice**) or trigger severe, life-threatening inflammation of the pancreas (**Acute Gallstone Pancreatitis**).

Why You Cannot Just Remove The Stones

A diseased gallbladder that produces stones has lost its healthy muscular contraction. Removing only the stones would leave behind a defective organ that rapidly generates new stones within months. Removing the entire gallbladder is the only permanent medical cure.

The Critical View of Safety (CVS) Protocol

Operating around the liver and biliary tree requires extreme precision. Dr. Atul Peters strictly follows the international **Critical View of Safety (CVS)** protocol—isolating and visually proving the cystic duct and artery before clipping, ensuring zero injury to vital liver structures.

Surgical Indications

When is Gallbladder Surgery Recommended?

Laparoscopic cholecystectomy is indicated for a range of acute and chronic biliary conditions diagnosed via Ultrasound.

01

Symptomatic Gallstones (Cholelithiasis)

Patients experiencing recurrent episodes of upper right abdominal pain, indigestion, bloating, nausea, or back pain after eating heavy or fried meals.

02

Acute & Chronic Cholecystitis

Active inflammation or thickening of the gallbladder wall caused by stone impaction. Urgent surgery prevents gallbladder empyema (pus accumulation) or gangrene.

03

Gallbladder Polyps (> 10 mm)

Polyps or fleshy growths inside the gallbladder larger than 1 centimeter carry a recognized risk of malignant transformation (gallbladder cancer) and warrant timely removal.

04

History of Gallstone Pancreatitis

Patients who have recovered from an episode of gallstone pancreatitis or biliary sludge migration should undergo cholecystectomy to prevent a recurrent, potentially fatal attack.

Common Questions

Get clear, evidence-based answers to the most frequently asked questions about Laparoscopic Cholecystectomy surgery.

Ask Dr. Peters
Can gallstones be treated with lasers or medicines without removing the gallbladder?

No. There is no laser, shockwave lithotripsy, or oral medicine that can dissolve or remove gallstones permanently without high recurrence rates. Furthermore, attempting to break stones can cause small fragments to slip into the common bile duct, leading to severe complications like **Jaundice** or **Acute Pancreatitis**. Because the diseased gallbladder is the source of stone formation, removing the entire organ (cholecystectomy) is the only international medical standard.

Will I gain weight or suffer chronic diarrhea after losing my gallbladder?

No. Gallbladder removal causes **zero changes to your metabolic rate or weight**. While a very small percentage of patients (< 5%) may notice slightly looser stools when eating very greasy meals during the first few weeks, the liver and small intestine adapt rapidly. Once healed, over 98% of patients eat a completely normal diet without digestive discomfort or diarrhea.

What is the difference between laparoscopic and robotic cholecystectomy?

Both are minimally invasive keyhole procedures performed through tiny incisions. While standard laparoscopy uses straight rigid instruments and 4K cameras, **Robotic Cholecystectomy (da Vinci Xi System)** provides 10x magnified 3D HD vision and articulating wristed instruments. Robotic surgery is particularly advantageous for severe chronic inflammation, dense scar tissue from previous surgeries, or complex biliary anatomy.

How soon can I shower and bathe after laparoscopic surgery?

We apply modern waterproof dressings over your 4 tiny keyhole incisions. You can take a quick shower **48 hours after surgery**, gently patting the dressings dry with a clean towel. Avoid soaking in a bathtub or swimming pool until your follow-up checkup at 1 week.

Is laparoscopic gallbladder surgery covered by health insurance in India?

Yes! Laparoscopic cholecystectomy for gallstones, cholecystitis, or polyps is covered under 100% of health insurance policies, corporate mediclaim, TPAs, and CGHS/ECHS schemes in India. Max Smart Hospital Saket provides comprehensive cashless claim assistance.

When can I resume driving, gym workouts, and office work?

You can resume driving and routine non-strenuous desk work within **3 to 5 days** once you are off prescription analgesics. Light walking is encouraged immediately. For heavy gym lifting, abdominal core exercises, running, or contact sports, we advise waiting **2 to 3 weeks** to allow your abdominal muscle planes to heal completely.
