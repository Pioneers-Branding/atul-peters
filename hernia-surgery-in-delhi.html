Anatomical Defect

What is a Hernia & Why Does It Occur?

A hernia occurs when there is a mechanical structural defect, weakness, or tear in the tough muscular layer (fascia) of your abdomen or groin. This allows internal tissues, such as omental fat or loops of intestine, to push through the gap and form a noticeable bulge.

Common Symptoms & Warning Signs

Early hernias may only appear during activities that increase intra-abdominal pressure, such as coughing, lifting heavy weights, or straining during bowel movements. However, symptoms progressively worsen over time:

  • Visible Bulge or Swelling: A soft lump in the groin, belly button (umbilicus), or along a previous surgical scar that disappears when lying flat.
  • Aching & Heavy Sensation: Chronic dull ache, pressure, or dragging sensation around the defect after prolonged standing or exertion.
  • Inability to Push Bulge Back: An incarcerated hernia occurs when the trapped bulge can no longer be gently massaged back inside.
  • Severe Pain, Vomiting & Redness: Emergency red-flag signs indicating a Strangulated Hernia where intestinal blood supply is cut off.

Hernias never close on their own. Early elective keyhole surgery prevents emergency strangulation.

Why Do Muscles Tear? Key Risk Factors

Abdominal wall tears occur due to either congenital weakness present from birth or acquired degeneration of collagen over adult life. Common triggers that stretch and rupture the muscular fascia include:

  • Heavy Physical Lifting & Straining: Repeated lifting of heavy weights or occupational strain increases internal abdominal pressure.
  • Chronic Cough & Constipation: Persistent coughing from smoking/COPD or straining due to chronic constipation or enlarged prostate.
  • Previous Open Abdominal Surgeries: Incisions from past C-sections, open appendix, or intestinal surgery can weaken healing scar tissues.
  • Obesity & Pregnancy: Excess body weight and multiple pregnancies place immense, continuous tension across the abdominal midline.

Diagnostic Protocol: While clinical examination identifies most hernias, Dr. Peters recommends a Dynamic Abdominal Wall Ultrasound or Multislice CECT Abdomen to map exact defect sizes before complex reconstruction.

Stage 1 • Elective Window

Reducible Hernia

The soft lump appears only during standing, coughing, or straining. When you lie down flat or press gently, the intestinal loop slides safely back into the abdominal cavity.

Action: Plan day-care eTEP surgery
Stage 2 • Urgent Care

Incarcerated Hernia

The muscular gap shrinks or the trapped omentum swells, preventing the bulge from going back inside even when lying flat. Causes chronic aching, heavy dragging, and severe discomfort.

Action: Consult surgeon immediately
Stage 3 • Medical Emergency

Strangulated Gangrenous

The tight muscle ring acts like a tourniquet, cutting off arterial blood supply to the trapped intestine. Causes rapid tissue death (gangrene), vomiting, severe pain, and septic shock within hours.

Action: Immediate emergency surgery required

Sub-lay Extraperitoneal Mastery

Why eTEP & TAR Are the International Gold Standard

In eTEP (extended Totally Extraperitoneal) and TAR (Transversus Abdominis Release) surgery, the surgeon creates a spacious natural anatomical pocket precisely between the layers of the abdominal wall muscles—completely outside the peritoneal cavity holding your bowel loops:

1

Zero Mesh-to-Bowel Contact

Because the prosthetic mesh is positioned inside the retro-muscular sub-lay space behind your rectus muscles, it never touches intestinal loops, ensuring zero risk of bowel adhesions or fistulas.

2

Elimination of Painful Peritoneal Tacks

Natural intra-abdominal pressure presses the muscles firmly against the sub-lay mesh like a sandwich. This eliminates the need for traumatic metallic or absorbable tacks, preventing chronic postoperative nerve pain.

3

Dynamic Abdominal Wall Restoration

By releasing tight myofascial planes (TAR), we bring wide muscle gaps back together in the midline, restoring the natural core strength and flat contour of your abdomen.

Swift Recovery

Your Hernia Surgery Recovery Timeline

Because eTEP and TAR keyhole techniques respect natural muscle layers without cutting through muscle fibers, recovery is rapid, comfortable, and predictable.

Step 01 • Day of Surgery

Admission & Keyhole Procedure

You get admitted to Max Hospital, Saket on the morning of surgery. Under general anesthesia, Dr. Peters performs the eTEP / TAR reconstruction through 3 to 4 tiny 5mm-10mm keyhole incisions. Operating time ranges from 60 to 120 minutes depending on defect size.

Step 02 • 4 Hours Post-Op

Early Walking & Oral Fluids

Within 4 hours of surgery, our ERAS nursing team assists you to stand up and walk. Because there are no painful tacks inside your abdominal cavity, pain levels are remarkably low. You start sips of water and warm soup the same evening.

Step 03 • Days 1 to 2

Hospital Discharge (24-48 Hours)

Patients with routine inguinal or primary ventral hernias are discharged within 24 hours. Patients with complex incisional reconstructions (TAR) stay comfortably for 48 hours before walking out with oral pain relief and waterproof dressings.

Step 04 • Days 5 to 7

Resume Desk Work & Driving

You can comfortably shower immediately after discharge. By day 5 to 7, you can drive, climb stairs, and resume routine desk work and light household activities without any discomfort.

Step 05 • Weeks 3 to 4

Mesh Incorporation & Walking

Over the first month, your body's natural collagen grows through the macro-porous prosthetic mesh, creating a reinforced biological shield. You can comfortably engage in brisk walking, light jogging, and active social routines.

Step 06 • Month 3

Return to Heavy Gym & Sports

By 8 to 12 weeks, the abdominal wall is fully healed and reinforced. After a final checkup with Dr. Peters, you can safely return to heavy weightlifting, core exercises, swimming, and competitive sports.
